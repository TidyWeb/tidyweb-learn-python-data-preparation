Field Reference

Data Analytics — Field Overview

This page is a conceptual map of the data analytics field. It is meant to make the terrain legible before tools, formulas, and workflows start arriving in detail. The aim is not to turn every topic into a specialist course. The aim is to show what each part of the field is for, how the parts connect, and why the same project often moves across several disciplines before a useful decision is made.

NOTE 01

What Data Analytics Is and Isn't

Definitiona working discipline

Data analytics is the disciplined practice of turning raw records into useful understanding for a real decision. It begins with messy evidence and ends with a clearer answer to a business, operational, scientific, or public question.

That answer is rarely just a number. It usually includes context, caveats, comparison, and an argument about what the evidence does and does not justify.

Data analytics is not just counting things. It is evidence work for decisions.
What the job really involvesweek-to-week reality

The title data analyst sounds as if the work begins at analysis and stays there. In reality, much of the week is spent clarifying the question, checking the data source, cleaning broken records, aligning definitions, validating results, rebuilding charts, and rewriting explanations for non-technical readers.

A working analyst often moves back and forth between detective, translator, skeptic, and communicator. The spreadsheet or dashboard is only the visible surface of a larger reasoning process.

Neighbouring rolessame ecosystem, different jobs
Role Main concern Typical output
Data analytics What happened, why it happened, and what it means for action now Findings, dashboards, decision support, recommendations
Data science Deeper modelling, experimentation, statistical inference, and more complex prediction Models, experiments, advanced methods, research-style findings
Data engineering How data is collected, moved, stored, transformed, and kept reliable Pipelines, data infrastructure, warehousing, stable sources
Machine learning Pattern-finding systems that generalise to new data Trained models, predictions, classification systems
These roles overlap, but they are not interchangeable.
Why organisations need analystsdecision support

Organisations need analysts because modern systems produce more data than intuition alone can absorb. Leaders want to know what customers are doing, which process is failing, whether a campaign worked, where money is leaking, what demand looks like next month, and which risks deserve attention first.

Analysts help convert scattered records into prioritised understanding. They reduce guesswork, expose hidden patterns, and make trade-offs more visible.

Four analytics typesquestion to decision ladder
Type Core question Example
Descriptive What happened? Sales fell by 12% last quarter.
Diagnostic Why did it happen? Sales fell mainly in one region after stock shortages.
Predictive What is likely to happen next? Demand is likely to stay weak next month if supply stays unstable.
Prescriptive What should we do? Shift inventory earlier and prioritise the region with the strongest recovery.
The field moves from seeing the past to guiding action.
NOTE 02

The Data Pipeline

Pipeline viewraw data to decision

The pipeline is the full journey from reality to decision. Something happens in the world, a system records it, the records are stored, cleaned, analysed, visualised, discussed, and finally turned into action.

The important idea is that a wrong decision at the end may come from a fault much earlier in the chain. A beautiful dashboard cannot repair bad collection, missing context, or broken definitions.

Stage by stagewhat each step does
Stage Purpose What can go wrong
Collection Capture events, transactions, answers, or sensor readings Missing fields, biased sampling, inconsistent definitions
Storage Keep data somewhere reliable and retrievable Duplicated records, poor schema design, access confusion
Cleaning Repair obvious defects so the data can be trusted Silent data loss, bad fill choices, inconsistent formats
Analysis Ask structured questions and build evidence Wrong metric choice, false comparison, overconfident claims
Visualisation Make patterns and contrasts easier to see Misleading axes, poor chart choice, decorative clutter
Decision Use the evidence to choose an action Ignoring caveats, over-trusting weak data, political misuse
Data shapesstructured vs unstructured

Structured data is organised into a predictable shape such as rows and columns. It is easier to sort, filter, group, and summarise because each field has a known place.

Unstructured data does not arrive in neat table form. Emails, documents, call recordings, photographs, chat logs, and video are all rich in meaning but harder to query directly.

Much real-world work involves either turning unstructured material into a more structured form or combining the two carefully.

Tables are easier to analyse, but much of the world does not arrive as a table.
Formats in plain EnglishCSV, JSON, API

CSV: a simple flat file where each row is one record and commas separate the fields. Good for moving tabular data around, but not ideal for complex nested structure.

JSON: a flexible text format that can hold lists, labels, and nested structure. Common when systems exchange richer data.

API: not a file format but a controlled way for one system to ask another for data or services. An API is more like a doorway or contract than a saved document.

Cleaning time cautionwhy analysis feels delayed

Cleaning often takes more time than the visible analysis because reality is untidy. Fields are missing, dates are inconsistent, categories drift, identifiers are reused, and source systems rarely agree perfectly.

When cleaning is skipped or rushed, the final analysis may look precise while resting on shaky foundations.

Most frustration in analytics comes from imperfect data, not from difficult charts.
Version controlwhat problem Git solves

Version control exists because analysis changes over time. Questions shift. Definitions evolve. Old results need checking. Teammates need to see what changed and why.

Git gives a project memory for files. It helps analysts compare versions, reverse bad edits, trace the source of a result, and collaborate without turning the work into a folder full of conflicting copies.

NOTE 03

The Toolkit Landscape

Group 1data handling

This group is about getting hold of data, shaping it, asking direct questions of it, and preparing it for the rest of the workflow.

Pythongeneral-purpose language

Python is a readable programming language that became central to analytics because it can glue many parts of the workflow together. It is not just for data, but data work is one of the reasons it became so widely adopted.

pandastable-shaped data work

pandas is the library analysts use when they want Python to behave more like a table tool. It supports filtering, grouping, aggregation, cleaning, reshaping, and summary work on structured data.

NumPyfast numerical computing

NumPy provides fast numerical arrays and the mathematical backbone underneath much of the Python data ecosystem. Analysts may not always touch it directly at first, but many tools depend on it.

scipystatistical tests and scientific computing

scipy extends NumPy with tools for scientific and statistical work. In analytics, the part most commonly used is scipy.stats, which provides hypothesis tests including the t-test.

pandas can describe data. scipy.stats can test it.

scipy is where the statistics moves from description to evidence testing.
SQLdatabase questioning language

SQL is the language used to ask questions of relational databases. It is older than Python, separate from Python, and still essential because most organisational data lives in database systems rather than loose files.

Group 2visualisation and communication

This group helps turn analysis into visible evidence that other people can inspect, compare, and discuss.

Matplotlibfoundational charting library

Matplotlib is the foundational charting layer in Python. It gives low-level control over charts and sits underneath many higher-level visual tools.

Seabornhigher-level statistical charting

Seaborn builds on Matplotlib and makes many statistical chart types easier to create cleanly. It is often chosen when the analyst wants polished comparisons quickly.

Power BIinteractive dashboards

Power BI is a business intelligence tool that lets analysts build dashboards and share results with decision-makers who may never read a notebook or a script.

Streamlitanalysis into lightweight apps

Streamlit is used when an analysis needs a simple interactive front end. It helps turn Python work into something colleagues can explore without reading the underlying workflow.

Group 3workflow and collaboration

This group is about how analysts think, explain, record, and share their work while it is still evolving.

Jupyter Notebooksworking document for analysis

Jupyter Notebooks combine explanation, executable work, outputs, and charts in one place. They are useful for exploration, teaching, prototypes, and transparent reasoning.

Gitlocal change tracking

Git keeps a structured history of how files changed over time. It gives analysts a disciplined way to revise work without losing earlier states.

GitHubshared repository platform

GitHub sits on top of Git and makes collaboration easier through sharing, review, discussion, and project visibility.

Group 4machine learning

This group becomes important when the goal shifts from description and explanation toward automated prediction or pattern learning.

Scikit-Learnclassical machine learning library

Scikit-Learn is the standard Python library for many machine learning workflows such as classification, regression, clustering, model evaluation, and preprocessing.

Not every analyst becomes a machine learning practitioner, but analysts benefit from understanding where ML fits.
NOTE 04

Statistics as a Language

Why statistics mattersmore than counting

Counting tells you how many. Statistics helps tell you what is typical, what is unusual, how spread out the values are, how much uncertainty is attached to a claim, and whether an observed pattern may just be noise.

It is a language for describing evidence under uncertainty.

Centre measuresmean, median, mode

Mean: the average. Useful when every value should influence the centre, but sensitive to extreme values.

Median: the middle value after sorting. More robust when the data contains outliers or strong skew.

Mode: the most common value. Useful when frequency matters more than the middle.

These three words all describe “centre,” but they do not mean the same kind of centre.
Spread and shapewhat the centre cannot show

A centre measure alone can hide the character of the data. Range gives the span from lowest to highest. Standard deviation gives a sense of how tightly or loosely the values sit around the mean.

Distribution shape asks a different question again: do values cluster, lean, split into groups, or stretch heavily to one side? Two datasets can share the same mean and still feel completely different once you see their shape.

Probability and testingreasoning under uncertainty

Probability is the language of likelihood. It helps analysts reason about how plausible different outcomes are and whether an observed pattern may simply be random variation.

Hypothesis testing adds a formal structure: make a claim, define the competing default position, compare the evidence to that default, and judge how surprising the observed result would be if the default were true.

p-value cautionnot a verdict machine

A p-value is not a truth detector, not a guaranteed measure of importance, and not a magical pass-fail stamp. It is a statement about how surprising the observed result would be under a particular null hypothesis.

If the underlying question is weak, the data is biased, or the assumptions are poor, a tidy p-value does not rescue the conclusion.

A p-value is one clue inside an argument, not the whole argument.
Significance vs importancestatistical vs practical
Question What it asks Why both matter
Statistical significance Is the pattern unlikely to be random under the chosen assumptions? A tiny effect can clear a statistical threshold in a large dataset.
Practical importance Is the size of the effect large enough to matter in the real world? A result can be real yet still too small to justify action.
Sample vs populationwhat the data stands for

A population means the full set of cases you care about. A sample means you only have some of them. Most datasets available to analysts are samples, even when they look large.

That means many conclusions are provisional. They describe the evidence you have directly and suggest something about the wider world, but they do not settle it beyond doubt.

NOTE 05

Machine Learning Without the Mysticism

What machine learning ispattern finding that generalises

Machine learning is about building systems that learn patterns from past data and use those patterns on new, unseen cases. The central test is not whether the system remembers the training data. The central test is whether it generalises.

Traditional programming vs MLwhere the rules come from
Approach Human provides System produces
Traditional programming Explicit rules Outputs after following those rules
Machine learning Examples and a learning setup A model that infers rules from the examples
In ordinary programming the rules are hand-written. In ML the rules are learned from data.
Learning typessupervised and unsupervised

Supervised learning uses labelled data, where the correct answer is already known for past examples. The system learns the relationship between inputs and that known answer.

Unsupervised learning does not begin with known answer labels. The system looks for structure, clusters, or underlying patterns without being told what the final grouping should be.

Core prediction tasksclassification, regression, clustering

Classification: predict which category something belongs to.

Regression: predict a numeric value.

Clustering: group similar items together without pre-existing labels.

Training and testingwhy the split matters

Data is split because evaluating a model on the same cases it learned from tells you almost nothing about whether it will work on new data. The model must be judged on cases it has not already seen.

Overfitting cautionmemorising instead of learning

Overfitting happens when a model learns the training data too closely, including noise and accident, and then performs badly on fresh cases. It can look brilliant during training and disappointing in reality.

A model that knows the past perfectly may still fail the future.
Neural networkswhere they sit

Neural networks are one family of machine learning models. Conceptually, they are layers of connected units that learn to transform inputs into outputs through many small weighted adjustments.

They are powerful for complex tasks, but they do not replace the need for simpler models, careful evaluation, clean data, or good problem framing.

Evaluation metricsno single score tells all

Accuracy: how often the model is correct overall.

Precision: when the model predicts a positive case, how often that prediction is truly positive.

Recall: of all the true positive cases available, how many the model successfully found.

RMSE: a common way to measure the average size of prediction error in numeric forecasting tasks.

Different mistakes matter differently, so the “best” metric depends on the real-world cost of being wrong.
NOTE 06

SQL and Relational Databases

What a database isproduction storage

A database is a managed system for storing, organising, and retrieving data at scale. Organisations use databases instead of loose CSV files because production data changes constantly, needs controlled access, and must remain reliable under many users and processes.

Relational logicmultiple connected tables

A relational database stores information across multiple tables that connect through shared identifiers. One table might describe customers, another orders, another products. The relationship lets you connect them when answering richer questions.

Relational does not mean “large.” It means the tables are linked by design.
What SQL isdeclarative questioning

SQL is a declarative language. That means you say what data you want, not every low-level step for how the system must fetch it. You describe the target result, and the database engine works out an execution plan.

Why analysts need SQL as well as Pythondifferent contexts

SQL is strongest close to the database, where large tables already live. Python is stronger when the analyst wants flexible transformation, richer custom logic, modelling, automation, or visual output beyond a database query.

Conceptual query flowhow a database question is shaped

Select: what information do you want back?

From: which table or source should it come from?

Where: which rows should be included or excluded?

Group by: when should many rows be collapsed into summaries?

Join: when do you need to connect information from separate tables?

What a join doesconnecting tables

A join combines rows from two tables based on a shared identifier. It is the conceptual move that lets a system say, for example, “take the order records and attach the customer details that match each one.”

SQL vs pandassame task, different place
Question SQL is often better when pandas is often better when
Filter, group, join, summarise The data already lives in a database and is too large or shared to pull casually into memory The analyst is already working locally and wants flexible manipulation, exploration, or follow-on modelling
SQL and pandas are not rivals so much as partners at different stages of the same job.
NOTE 07

Ethics, Bias, and the Real World

Where problems really come frombad assumptions, not just bad maths

Data work can fail even when the calculations are correct. If the data excludes certain groups, reflects biased historical decisions, encodes unfair categories, or hides important context, the final analysis may be technically tidy and still socially harmful.

Bias cautionthe data can be the problem

Bias means the evidence does not represent the wider reality fairly. This can happen through who gets measured, how labels are assigned, which outcomes are recorded, or which cases were historically given more attention than others.

Historical bias is especially dangerous because a model can quietly learn yesterday's unfairness and reproduce it tomorrow at scale.

A model can be mathematically sound and still be unfair.
GDPR in plain Englishdata rights and limits

GDPR is a legal framework that says personal data cannot be treated as a free raw material. People have rights over how their data is collected, used, stored, and deleted.

In plain terms, analysts must know why the data is being used, whether that use is allowed, whether people consented where needed, and whether a person can ask for correction or removal.

Data governanceownership, access, accountability

Data governance is the system of rules and responsibilities around who owns data, who can access it, how it is documented, and who is accountable when it is misused or misunderstood.

Biased model vs broken modeldifferent failure modes

Broken model: produces poor predictions because it does not work technically.

Biased model: may work technically by its own metric and still produce systematically unfair outcomes for certain groups.

Responsible practicewhat a good team does

Responsible teams document data sources, define metrics clearly, state limitations openly, question representativeness, and avoid presenting uncertain findings as settled truth.

They treat transparency as part of the work, not as an optional moral extra added after the charts are finished.

Responsible analytics means making the limits of the evidence visible, not hiding them.
